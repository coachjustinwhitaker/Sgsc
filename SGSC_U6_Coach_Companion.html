U6 Coach Companion
Where It All Begins

Discovery Phase  ·  Small-Sided Format  ·  Recreational
Session Length:45–50 minutes total  ·  Arrive 15 minutes early

The most important thing that happens on this field is fun. Everything else is secondary.

Some of these players have never touched a soccer ball. Some have never been on a team. Some are going to cry. Some are going to score a goal and then run to their parents instead of back to the game. All of that is exactly right. Your job this season is simple: make this the best hour of their week. Kids who leave the field smiling, sweaty, and already asking when the next session is. That is the whole thing. Everything else, every skill and every concept that follows for the next decade of their development, starts with that.

US Soccer Alignment
Discovery Phase  ·  4v4 Format

US Soccer calls this age group Discovery Phase: create a love of the game through joyful, active, game-based experiences. Every activity in this guide is designed with that in mind. You do not need tactical knowledge to deliver a great U6 session. You need energy, enthusiasm, and a willingness to hiss like a snake.

Who Is In Front of You
Understanding Your U6 Players

They Are Just Starting

For many of these players, this is their first experience on a team. Some have never kicked a ball. Start with zero assumptions about what they know. Everything is new and that is a gift. You get to be the person who introduces them to the game.

Fun Is the Curriculum

At this age, fun is not a means to an end. It is the end. A player who loves being on the field at age 6 is a player who is still playing at 16. A player who has a bad experience now may never come back. That is the real stakes here.

Movement Matters More Than You Think

The hopping, the turning, the chasing, the changing direction. These are not just games. They are building the coordination, balance, and agility that underpin every athletic skill your players will ever develop. The research on this is clear: varied movement play at this age has measurable benefits for athletic development years down the line. The animal games are doing real work, even when it looks like pure chaos.

Short Attention Spans Are Normal

A six-year-old has about 6 to 8 minutes of focused attention before they need a reset. This is not misbehavior, it is neuroscience. Keep activities short. Switch often. If something is not working, just move on. You do not need to fix it.

A note on the animal games: These might look like pure silliness and they are, on purpose. But every single one is also developing something real. Hopping with two feet. Lateral cuts. Stopping and starting quickly. Moving in sync with another person. Tracking multiple things at once. These are the foundational movement skills that coaches at U10, U12, and beyond will be grateful their players already have. You do not need to tell the players any of that. Just run the game and let them be ridiculous.

What a Great U6 Session Looks Like
Constant movement

Players are running, chasing, dribbling, hopping, or laughing for almost every minute of the session. If players are standing still waiting, the game needs to change.

Noise and joy

The louder and more chaotic it sounds, the better. Squealing, giggling, shouting animal names. This is exactly what success sounds like at U6.

Every player has a ball

During practice activities, every player should have their own ball at their feet as much as possible. Waiting in line or sharing kills engagement at this age.

Lots of goals

Set up games where players score frequently. Nothing builds confidence and excitement faster than scoring a goal. Celebrate every single one.

The coach is playing too

At U6, you are not standing on the sideline with a clipboard. You are the shark. You are Wreck-It Ralph. Get in there. Your energy sets the tone for the whole session.

It ends too soon

If players are asking “can we play more?” when the session ends, you have done your job perfectly. End while they still want more, every single week.

Three Things — That Is All
The Whole Curriculum for U6

There are no player action frameworks, no tactical concepts, and no positional requirements at this age. Just three things, said plainly, that you can come back to in any game at any moment.

1
Move with the ball
Keep it close. Use your feet. Take it with you.
2
Heads up
See what is around you. Find space. See your teammates.
3
Have fun
This is the most important one. It is also the one coaches forget most often.
Before You Arrive
What Prepared Looks Like
Preparation at U6 is simple. Know which two or three activities you are going to run. Have your cones laid out before the first player arrives. Put a ball at every player's starting spot. That is it. You do not need a session plan with timing breakdowns. You need to show up with energy and an idea of what game you are playing first. The rest follows.
The Parents on the Sideline

U6 parents are more anxious than any other age group, and more likely to intervene. Before the session, walk over and say something like:

“These sessions are going to look like organized chaos, and that is completely intentional. The games are silly on purpose. Every one of them is building coordination, confidence, and a love of the ball. The best thing you can do is cheer loudly and let them figure things out for themselves. If they run to you mid-game, just point them back to the field with a smile.”

At this age, a crying player who came to the field is still a win. Stay warm, stay calm, and keep things moving. Most players warm up within a few sessions.

If you see a parent who is genuinely enthusiastic and engaged, that conversation is worth having. The club always has room for coaches who care.

Opening Play 8–10 min → Activity 1 10 min → Activity 2 10 min → Closing Play 10 min
Opening Play
Small-Sided Game — Free Play
Same setup every week. Let them run. Get out of the way.

The Setup

Mini or pop-up goals at each end if you have them, cone goals otherwise. Pop-up goals give players the satisfaction of the ball going into a net, which matters at this age. Split however many players you have into two roughly equal teams. No goalkeeper, no offside, no rules beyond kick-in restarts. Keep the field small enough that every player is near the action most of the time. When in doubt, go smaller. A crowded small field means more touches and more goals. Mark the space, put the ball down, and step back. Restart quickly after every goal and celebrate each one.

Your Job

Get in there a little. Encourage. High-five. React to goals like they are the greatest thing you have ever seen. At U6, your energy is contagious in both directions. If you are having fun, they are having fun. Do not coach. Do not correct. Just play.
⚽ 1 ball per team 🏈 Mini or pop-up goals (cone goals as fallback) 🟠 Disc cones for boundaries 🟡 Pinnies — 2 colors
Practice
Activity Toolkit — Pick Two Per Session

Run each activity for about 8 to 10 minutes, then switch. You do not need to explain the games at length. A quick demo is worth ten minutes of instruction. If a game is not working or energy drops, just move on to the next one. That is not failure. That is good coaching at this age.

ANIMAL & TAG GAMES  —  Movement, coordination, agility, and joy
Activity 01
Sharks and Minnows
Dribbling · Changing Direction · Agility
Setup
Mark a rectangle and call it the ocean. Every player is a minnow with their own ball. You are the shark. Minnows line up along one end and on your signal they all dribble to the other side without getting their ball knocked away by you. Any minnow whose ball is knocked out becomes a shark and joins you for the next crossing. Minnows who make it safely line up and go again. Play until everyone has become a shark, then start fresh with a new volunteer as the first shark.
What to Look For
Encourage minnows to keep their heads up to find gaps, small touches to get around the sharks, and bigger touches to escape when they have space. The game is self-regulating as more sharks appear. Go after your more confident players first.
“How did you get past the shark? Did you go fast or did you go around?”
1 ball per player  ·  Disc cones for rectangle
Activity 02
Octopus
Dribbling · Small Touches · Speed
Setup
Mark a square grid and call it the ocean. Every player has a ball and dribbles freely anywhere in the grid. You are the octopus, roaming the grid trying to tag players. If tagged, it is 10 toe taps to get back in. Hold two pool noodles as tentacles if you have them. They make no practical difference and players will absolutely love it. Unlike Sharks and Minnows, there is no crossing from side to side here: players move freely in all directions and must dodge you wherever they go.
What to Look For
Look for players using small touches to dodge around you and bigger touches to escape when they have space. Slow down for players who need more success, speed up for players who need more challenge. The free-roaming format means every player is always active, which keeps energy high.
“When I came toward you, how did you decide which way to go?”
1 ball per player  ·  Disc cones for grid  ·  2 pool noodles as tentacles (optional but highly recommended)
Activity 03
Kangaroo Jacks
Dribbling · Agility · Coordination
Setup
Rectangle about 20 by 15 yards. One or two volunteers are the Kangaroo Jacks. Everyone else has a ball. Kangaroo Jacks must hop everywhere they go. Players dribble from one side of the grid to the other without getting tagged by a hopping Kangaroo Jack. If tagged, that player becomes a Kangaroo too. After a minute or so, call time, catch your breath, and play again.
What to Look For
The hopping is the whole point. Two-footed hopping is genuinely difficult for many 5 and 6 year olds and they will fall over and think it is hilarious. That is fine. Let them. The coordination work happening in this game is foundational to athletic development and they will never know they are training. They just think they are being kangaroos.
“When more kangaroos appeared, how did you find space to dribble?”
1 ball per player (except kangaroos)  ·  Disc cones for rectangle
Activity 04
Snake
Communication · Coordination · Teamwork
Setup
Grid about 20 by 20 yards. One player is the head of the snake, the only player without a ball. Everyone else dribbles freely. The snake runs around trying to tag other players. When tagged, a player grabs the back of the snake's shirt and joins the snake. The snake grows with every tag. They must stay connected for a tag to count. The game ends when everyone has become part of the snake. Start again with a new volunteer as the head.
What to Look For
The snake will fall apart constantly. That is the fun. Encourage the snake to communicate and call out who they are going after. Remind non-snakes to keep their heads up to find snake-free space. Watch for moments of genuine teamwork as the snake gets longer as the group instinctively learns to coordinate. Hissing is encouraged and should be modeled by the coach.
“When you were in the snake, how did you decide which direction to go?”
1 ball per player (except the snake head)  ·  Disc cones for grid
Activity 05
Bunny Trail
Dribbling · Coordination · Hopping
Setup
Create a circle of cones spaced about 6 feet apart. Each player starts at a cone with their own ball. On your call, players either dribble to the next cone or hop while carrying their ball. Alternate between dribbling and hopping segments on your command. Tell players to imagine a force field the size of their arms and to keep the ball inside it while they dribble.
What to Look For
The dribbling and hopping alternate creates a natural rhythm and the coordination challenge of switching between the two is the whole point. Some players will struggle with two-footed hopping. Demonstrate it and encourage the attempt. Do not run more than three hopping segments in a row or energy will drop.
“Which was harder, dribbling or hopping? Why?”
1 ball per player  ·  1 cone per player arranged in a circle
Activity 06
Dinosaur Tag
Dribbling · Direction Changes · Pairs
Setup
Each pair sets up a triangle of three cones with sides about 5 yards long. One player is the dinosaur, one is the park ranger. Both dribble around their triangle. The dinosaur tries to tag the park ranger. The park ranger can go any direction around the triangle but cannot leave the ball behind. Switch roles after every tag or after one minute. Hissing by the dinosaur is strongly encouraged.
What to Look For
Some players will struggle to turn the ball around the triangle corners. That is fine. The skill develops with time on the ball. Focus on the competition and the fun, not the technique. The pairs format means everyone is active simultaneously and no one is waiting. Watch for players using both feet.
“What did you do when the dinosaur was catching up to you?”
1 ball per pair  ·  3 cones per pair
BALL SKILLS & SCORING  —  Time on the ball, shooting, and the joy of scoring
Activity 07
101 Dalmatians
Dribbling · Ball Control · Scoring
Setup
Grid about 25 by 20 yards with cone triangles scattered throughout, one per player. Every player has a ball. Players score by dribbling through a triangle with the ball within arm's reach of their body. Players count their own score out loud and shout it each time they score. Set a group target before the game starts and count up together. Try 20 to start. If they get there quickly, raise it next round. Your job is to roam the grid and knock balls away. Go after your strongest players first.
What to Look For
Count up loudly with the group as the number climbs. The shared team goal creates genuine collective excitement when they hit the target. Raise the number each round to keep it challenging. Encourage small touches and heads up. Do not worry about perfect technique. Just getting the ball to the triangles is a win at this age.
“How many did we get? Should we try for more next round?”
1 ball per player  ·  Disc cones for outer grid and inner triangles
Activity 08
Red Light Green Light
Driving · Ball Control · Listening
Setup
Grid about 25 by 20 yards. Every player has a ball. Green light: dribble fast. Yellow light: slow down with small touches. Red light: stop with one foot on the ball. Call lights at random. After a minute, ask players to suggest a new color and assign it a new action: orange means spin around, purple means jump. Add each new color a few times before introducing the next one.
What to Look For
This game is simple, familiar, and brilliant at this age. Most players already know the concept from the road. The player-generated colors keep it fresh and give them ownership. Look for players keeping the ball within arm's reach at yellow and actually stopping on red rather than letting the ball roll away.
“What was the hardest color to remember? What action did you pick?”
1 ball per player  ·  Disc cones for grid
Activity 09
Wreck-It Ralph
Dribbling · Shooting · Decision-Making
Setup
Grid about 20 by 20 yards with a cone goal in each corner, opening inward. Every player has a ball. Players dribble around the grid and score by dribbling their ball through any corner goal. You are Wreck-It Ralph, roaming the grid and trying to knock balls away. Ball knocked out: 10 toe taps to re-enter. Play short rounds of about a minute. Encourage players to beat their own score.
What to Look For
Players who look up to find an open goal before driving toward it are already doing something sophisticated. Most will just head for the nearest goal, and that is completely fine at U6. Celebrate every goal loudly. The game introduces goal-scoring instinct without any coaching required.
“How many goals did you score? Which goal was easiest to get to?”
1 ball per player  ·  Disc cones for grid and corner goals
Activity 10
Mosquitoes
Dribbling · Shooting · Chaos
Setup
Small grid about 15 by 15 yards. Divide players into two groups: mosquitoes (each with a ball) and campers (no ball). Mosquitoes dribble their ball and try to hit campers below the knee. When a mosquito lands a hit, that player switches roles. Play until you call time. Keep the grid small so players have to dribble close before striking rather than shooting from distance.
What to Look For
The game is controlled chaos and that is the point. It combines dribbling, shooting, and spatial awareness in a genuinely silly format that 6-year-olds absolutely love. Watch for players who actually dribble close before shooting rather than kicking from far away. Keep the space tight. This focuses the game and reduces wild shots.
“How did you sneak up on your camper without them getting away?”
Coach NoteKeep the grid small enough that players must dribble in close before shooting. A smaller space naturally keeps shots below knee height. If balls are consistently going high, narrow the space further or switch to a different game.
1 ball per mosquito  ·  Disc cones for grid
Closing Play
Small-Sided Game — Back to Play
Same as the opening. Let it run. End while they still want more.

The Setup

Same small-sided game as the opening. Mini or pop-up goals if you have them, cone goals otherwise. No goalkeeper, no offside. Same teams or mix them up, either works. Let it run without interruption. Your job is to restart quickly after goals and keep the energy up.

End the Session Well

Bring everyone in at the end. High-fives all around. One question, very simple, just to close the loop.
Closing Question — Ask it at the end of every session “What was your favorite part of today?”
⚽ 1 ball per team 🏈 Mini or pop-up goals (cone goals as fallback) 🟠 Disc cones for boundaries 🟡 Pinnies — 2 colors
Coaching Cues
What to Say and When to Say It

At U6, coaching cues are less about technique and more about keeping energy, confidence, and participation high. These are the moments you will encounter most often and what to do with them.

Player is standing still or disengaged

Go to them. Get a ball at their feet. Get them moving. Do not ask what is wrong in front of the group. Just play alongside them for a minute.

Player is upset or crying

Kneel down, speak quietly, and give them a moment. Do not rush them back in. Most players come back on their own when they are ready. Keep the game going so they have something to come back to.

Player runs to their parents mid-game

Normal. Completely normal. Let the parent redirect them back. If it happens every session, have a quiet word with the parent about how to respond.

Player scores a goal

React like it is the greatest thing you have ever seen. Name the player. Make the celebration visible. At U6, that moment of recognition is more valuable than any coaching point.

Game is getting too chaotic

That is usually fine. If it genuinely breaks down, call a quick freeze, give one simple instruction, and restart. Do not stop the game for more than 30 seconds.

Players are not listening to instructions

Keep instructions to one sentence. Demonstrate rather than explain. If they are not listening, start the game and they will figure it out. Playing is more important than understanding the rules perfectly.

Session Setup
What You'll Need Each Session
Balls — 1 per player (Size 3) Every player needs their own ball during practice activities. This is the one non-negotiable.
Disc Cones — 20 per team For the game field boundaries, triangles, and grids. More than enough for all activities in this guide.
Tall Cones — 6 to 8 per team For cone goals and gates. Pairs of tall cones an arm's length apart make a goal. Multiple colors add a fun directional cue.
Pinnies — Full squad, 2 colors For the small-sided game. Also useful as octopus tentacles and duck tails in animal games.
Pool noodles (optional) Two pool noodles for the octopus tentacles are completely optional but will produce a reaction that makes the entire session worthwhile.
Player Development
Where Your Players Will Be

These are not performance standards. They are signs that development is happening. Every player moves at their own pace and not every player will show all of these by the end of the season. That is completely normal.

A Player Who Had a Great U6 Season

Runs toward the ball instead of away from it
Moves with the ball rather than kicking it away
Knows where the goal is and tries to shoot at it
Shows up to the next session excited to play again
That last one is the most important.
