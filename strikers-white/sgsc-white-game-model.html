01

The Game Model — Three Pillars

Our Identity

These three pillars are the foundation of how we play and who we are. They are not separate ideas. They are layered. Smart informs Competent, which enables Purpose. Every decision on the field flows from this foundation.

Smart · Competent · Purpose
Pillar 01
Smart
We play with an understanding of the game, the rules, of our opponent and of ourselves. We see how our opponent plays and we shift and adapt to find areas to exploit. We understand each other's skills and weaknesses. We work together to build each other up and to always support each other.
Pillar 02
Competent
We play with confidence knowing that our skills have been honed through experience and training. We understand the game, how others play it, and how we use our skills on the field. Competency is a lifelong staircase. Every step lifts us further, and we have many more to go.
Pillar 03
Purpose
We play with purpose. Every decision on the field has a reason behind it. We are intentional in how we press, how we transition, how we build, and how we attack. We are patient when patience creates the opportunity, and direct when directness wins the moment. We know when, where, and how to strike.
02

Principles of Play — Four Moments

Every game moves through four moments: Out of Possession, Transition Ball Won, Transition Ball Lost, and In Possession. Each moment is organized across three zones of the field. Every zone has a leader, a formation, and a set of principles that tell us how to play in that situation. The system is fluid. The formation shifts as the ball moves and as the situation changes. That fluidity is the point.

01
Out of Possession
▾
Universal Principle
We defend as a unit. Every player has a role in every defensive moment. There is no standing and watching. Press together, recover together, hold together.
3–5–2
Their Defensive Third
Zone Leader
The 10
Trigger
Goal kick or deep pass back to CB or GK activates the press immediately. Both strikers go on the trigger. No hesitation.
Press Shape — Four Lines
The press operates in four lines. Line 1: both strikers apply direct pressure and cut the central lane, forcing play wide. Line 2: the 10 and 8 support the press, closing space and cutting secondary passing options. Line 3: the 6 and wingbacks hold shape, compress space, and anticipate the wide outlet. Line 4: the back three stays compact and does not step out to chase.
Backline and GK
Back three stay compact. Center CB holds fractionally deeper as a sweeper. GK plays higher to cover the ball over the top.
Press Broken
Midfield delays to allow strikers to recover. Backline snaps flat to hold the offside trap.
Adapt
If direct balls consistently bypass the press in the first 10–15 minutes, GK or center CB calls the shift to a mid-block. Their call, not the sideline.
3–5–2
Middle Third
Zone Leader
The 6 — calls unit shifts and trap triggers
Shape
Compress vertically. Gap between midfield and backline stays tight, roughly 15 to 20 yards. Mids flatten into a compact block. No space through the middle.
Controlled Pressure
Relax the press. Allow lateral and backward passing on their half. We are patient, waiting for the mistake, not chasing the ball.
Unit Shifting
The midfield block shifts together as a unit. One midfielder out of position creates the gap. Collective movement is non-negotiable.
Transition Readiness
Strikers stay connected to the midfield line, not dropping deep. The moment the ball is won, they are immediate outlets.
5–3–2
Our Defensive Third
Zone Leader
GK — takes full command of the defensive third
Shape — Low Block
Wingbacks absorb into the defensive line creating a back five. Three mids hold a compact line above. Strikers drop into the gap between the mid and defensive lines to pressure opposing mids from behind.
Aerial Duels and Wide Coverage
CBs own the air. Opposite-side wingback tracks and covers back post runners. Strikers hold their gap position.
Mid Pressure Trigger
Mids apply primary pressure. Wingback steps if ball goes wide. When a wingback steps, a mid slides across to cover. Ball goes back, restructure immediately.
Set Pieces
Corner kicks and dead ball situations follow set piece principles, handled separately from open play structure.
02
Transition — Ball Won
▾
Universal Principle
Win it, read the pressure, play forward at the right moment, at the right speed, to the right target. Attack is the default. Three seconds is the window before their shape recovers. Scan before you win the ball, not after. All positions must master receiving half-turned or facing forward. Receiving with back to goal under pressure is how dangerous turnovers happen.
3–5–2
Their Defensive Third
Zone Leader
The 10 — organizes the attack
Decision Tree — Forward First
Forward is always the first look. Can I shoot? Take it. Can I find the other striker in a better position? Play them. Are the wingbacks pushing into space? Find them. If none of those options are there, recycle to the midfield and create the next opportunity. Every decision starts with: can we go forward?
Strikers
Push high immediately. Position for shots, headers, and second balls. Rebounds are not luck. They are positioning. Second ball mentality is an expectation, not a bonus.
Wingbacks
Wingbacks are midfielders who own the wide channels. Hold width and time the forward run into dangerous space as the ball arrives. Timing the run is the skill.
The 6 — Safety Valve
Holds deepest of the mids. Primary recycle target. First defensive position if possession is lost. The pivot the system rotates around when transition reverses.
3–5–2
Middle Third
Zone Leader
The 6 — safety valve and transition anchor
Decision Tree
Can we play forward or drive into space on the wide channels? Can we find the striker centrally? If not, find width. If not, hold possession and build up. Forward first, always.
Drive Into Space, Not Pressure
Midfielders who win the ball centrally should drive forward if space exists. Carrying 10–15 yards forces defensive decisions. Drive into space. Never drive into traffic.
Read What the Defense Gives You
A defender committing to you is a gift to your teammates. Scan before the drive. 1v1 is a test. 1v3 with open teammates is a decision failure.
Wingbacks as Wide Outlets
Wingbacks are the first wide option, already positioned slightly ahead of the ball and ready to receive. Wide outlets stretch the defense and create the central space for the striker run.
5–3–2
Our Defensive Third
Zone Leader
Center CB initiates — GK supports distribution
Heavy Pressure — Clear It
Under heavy pressure in a danger zone, clear wide and long. If it goes out, the throw-in gives us time to reset. A clearance back to the opponent resets nothing.
Moderate Pressure — Find the Wide Lane
First look is whether a wingback can step out of the line into space immediately. If that lane is open, play them quickly and transition. This is the fastest way out of the low block.
Moderate Pressure — Find the 6 or 8
If the wide lane is covered, find the 6 or 8. They receive half-turned or facing forward, never with back to goal in our own third.
Light Pressure — Survey and Build
Survey the field. Decide which side to build on. Switch the ball across the CB line intentionally. Use the GK as an active field player to shift the point of attack.
03
Transition — Ball Lost
▾
Universal Principle
The moment possession ends, the nearest player pressures immediately. Everyone else recovers their shape. No ball watching, ever. The transition from attack to defense is not a pause. It is an action.
3–5–2
Their Defensive Third
Zone Leader
The 10 — reads the loss and organizes the immediate response
Immediate Pressure
Nearest player closes the ball carrier immediately, no hesitation. Force them to make a decision under pressure before they can transition.
The 6 Drops
The 6 reads the loss and drops toward the midfield line immediately to protect the central channel. This is the most important recovery movement in this zone.
Wingbacks Recover
Wingbacks sprint to recover their midfield position. They are not defenders yet, but the gap has to close fast. This is the most physically demanding read in the system and the wingbacks must be constantly aware of it.
Back Three Holds
The back three holds shape and does not step out to chase. Their job is to stay compact and be ready for what comes through the press.
3–5–2
Middle Third
Zone Leader
The 6 — compacts the unit and holds the center
Immediate Pressure
Nearest player applies pressure on the ball. Not a sprint past. A disciplined closing that delays the attack and forces the decision.
Unit Compacts
Midfield unit closes gaps and gets tight. Shape is already relatively intact in this zone. The response is about tightening, not recovering from distance.
Wingbacks Tuck In
Wingbacks tuck into the midfield line immediately. No lingering in advanced positions. The wide channels become defensive responsibilities first.
Back Three Holds
Back three maintains shape. Does not push forward. Provides the defensive foundation that the midfield recovery anchors against.
5–3–2
Our Defensive Third
Zone Leader
GK — takes immediate command
Immediate Compactness
Everyone behind the ball gets compact immediately. The 5-3-2 low block triggers automatically. There is no decision to make. The shape is the response.
GK Commands
GK takes full command, calling the line, directing shape, and communicating every action. This is the highest danger situation and the GK's voice is the anchor.
No Panic
Losing the ball in our own third is rare if our build-up principles are working. When it happens, composure is the first requirement. Shape, compact, communicate, defend.
04
In Possession
▾
Universal Principle
We possess the ball to create opportunity, not to count passes. Every circulation has a purpose: find the space to go forward, use the width to keep that space available, and commit to the attack when the moment is right. Recycling is hunting for the better attack.
3–2–5
Their Defensive Third
Zone Leader
The 10 — creative engine, final pass, timing
Overload and Create
We have numbers in this zone by design. Use them. Every player without the ball is moving to pull a defender out of position. Movement creates the gaps, not individual skill alone.
Patience With Purpose
We have the ball in a dangerous area. Do not rush the final action, but do not recycle sideways forever either. We are looking for the right moment to commit, not the perfect moment.
Strikers
Create space for each other. If one striker drops, the other runs in behind. Movement is constant. Standing still gives the defender an easy job.
Wingbacks
Provide width and the crossing or cutback option. Time the run into the box as the ball arrives. The run timing is the skill. Too early and the ball is not yet there. Too late and the moment is gone.
The 6 — Insurance
Holds at the top of the attack. Does not commit into the box. If possession is lost, the 6 is already in position to protect. The system does not work without this discipline.
3–5–2
Middle Third
Zone Leader
The 6 and 10 jointly — 8 connects and arrives late
The 6–8–10 Axis
The 6 provides the foundation and models patience. The 10 provides the vision and models ambition. The 8 connects them, the most physically demanding role, linking defensive stability to creative attack and arriving late into the box.
Two Reads
Is there a forward pass that puts us in a better position? Take it. No forward pass available? Recycle and create one. Both reads are correct. Recycling is hunting for the better attack, not a step backward.
Width Is Constant
The wide channels are always occupied. Wingbacks hold width at all times in this zone. Coming from smaller field formats, the space on a full field is real and available. Trust it and use it.
Verticality Is the Ambition
If there is a through ball for a runner, play it. If there is a combination to create a wide opportunity, play that. Find the space to move forward and use it. We circulate to find the forward pass, not to maintain possession for its own sake.
5–3–2
Our Defensive Third
Zone Leader
Center CB organizes build-up — GK supports distribution
No Panic, No Aimless Long Balls
Build intentionally. The CB reads the pressure, calls the direction, and controls the tempo. Every pass out of the back has a purpose.
GK as a Field Player
The GK is an active participant in build-up, providing the angle to switch sides, receiving under pressure, and redistributing with purpose. On turf this is a skill we develop and own.
Switch to Find the Better Side
Move the ball across the CB line intentionally to shift the opponent's pressure. Switching is a build-up tool, not a fallback. Find the side with space and attack from a position of control.
Get Forward When the Lane Opens
The wide lane is always the first look. If a wingback can step into space immediately, play them and transition quickly. If not, build through the backline until it opens.
