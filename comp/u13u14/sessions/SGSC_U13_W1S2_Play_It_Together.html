Philosophy Connection

Monday was about what you see before the ball arrives. Tonight takes that same habit and puts a teammate in the picture. Scanning for space is one skill. Scanning for a teammate's angle, their timing, whether they're giving you something useful to play into — that's the next layer. The wall pass is the simplest form of that idea: one pass, one return, one decision made faster because you already knew where your teammate was. Every combination we build this season starts here.

Session Objectives
  • Scan beyond the ball to identify a teammate's position and angle before receiving
  • Execute a clean wall pass: weight, surface, and body shape going into the pass sets up the return
  • Use the first touch on the return to move forward, not just to control
  • Move after the pass, not watch it
  • Recognize the basic decision: combine with a teammate or carry individually, based on what the picture shows

Activity Diagram
ObjectiveGet bodies moving while immediately demanding the habit we are building this week: scan before you receive. Two pugg goals per sideline means one goal opens as the other closes — players have to switch the point of attack laterally — players who scanned early know where to go. The tight 20×30 field makes individual carrying harder than it looks. A wall pass gets you past pressure faster than a dribble in this space. Let the field do some of the teaching before we name it.
Coach Note

No touch limits. Let it run free and watch what combination play emerges naturally. You are collecting data on who already looks for a teammate before receiving versus who puts their head down and carries. That distinction becomes the coaching point in Activity 3.

Coaching Points
  • Before you receive: which pugg is open right now?
  • Did you see your teammate before the ball arrived, or after?
  • Celebrate a combination that leads to a goal out loud — that is tonight's habit showing up live

Activity Diagram
ObjectiveInstall the clean mechanics of the wall pass before any live pressure is added. Three teachable moments: the pass to the wall (weight, surface, angle), the return and first touch (redirect forward, not just control), and the movement after (drive through, not watch). Two lines running simultaneously creates natural spatial awareness — players must read where the other driver is and time their approach — without adding a defender yet.
Action Sequence
Step 1Drive from the line toward the mannequin, reading where the opposite-line player is to time the approach. Players exit to the opposite line after each rep.
Step 2Play to the available wall player — firm pass to their feet, body shape already opening toward the return before the pass leaves the foot.
Step 3First touch on the return moves the ball forward into space, not just to the foot. Drive through and exit to the opposite line.
Coach Note

Being a wall player is its own technical skill. The wall player sets their angle so the return is easy to play forward, adjusts to give a good target, and times the touch so the ball arrives on the driver's stride. Name this explicitly when rotating players into wall positions: you are not resting, you are practicing the other half of the combination.

Coaching Points
  • Pass to the wall: is your body already opening toward the return before you play it?
  • First touch on the return: does it move you forward, or does it kill the combination?
  • Movement after the return: drive through, do not stop and watch
  • Wall players: call for the ball and show where you want it before it arrives, a clean wall pass starts with a clear target
  • Wall players: set your angle so the return is easy to play forward, not just back to their feet
  • Two lines: read where the other driver is before you enter the grid

Activity Diagram
ObjectiveTake the wall pass mechanic from Activity 3 and put it under live pressure for the first time. The mannequin did not make a decision — this defender does. The two wide outlets check to create angles rather than standing at cones, so the central player must scan for their movement before receiving. Three real options: play right outlet, play left outlet, or beat the defender 1v1 and drive to the small goal. The combination should emerge as the better solution most of the time because of numbers, not because the dribble has been removed.
Action Sequence
Step 1Ball fed in by coach. Central player drives at the live defender, reading the picture: where are the wide outlets checking to?
Step 2Decision: play right outlet and get the return, play left outlet and get the return, or beat the defender 1v1 and drive to the small goal.
Step 3After the combination: second decision — finish directly or play across to the opposite wide outlet before shooting.
Coach Note

Resist telling players which option to take. The decision is the point. If a player beats the defender 1v1 and scores, that was the right read. If they force a combination when the dribble was clearly on, ask what they saw that made them choose to combine. You want them reasoning, not following a pattern.

Coaching Points
  • Before you receive: where are the wide outlets right now, and which angle is useful?
  • Pass to the outlet: same weight and surface as the wall pass grid — this is the same action under pressure
  • First touch on the return: forward, not just controlled
  • Wide outlets: check to create the angle, do not stand and wait for the ball to find you
  • What told you to combine or carry — something you saw, or a guess?

Activity Diagram
ObjectiveThe combination play from Activities 3 and 4 now has to work against three live defenders with a real finishing objective. The wide jokers are permanently available to the attacking team but wall pass only, one or two touches back to the central player who used them. The 3v3 is tight enough that the combination through a joker is often the best solution to breaking the defensive block centrally. When the defending team wins the ball they counter to two pugg goals at the opposite end, creating a real transition moment in both directions.
Rules
JokersPlay for the attacking team only. One or two touches, wall pass return only — no carrying, no shooting. Rotate joker roles every few minutes so everyone gets the wall player job.
ScoringAttacking team shoots on full goal. Defending team wins the ball and counters to either pugg goal at the opposite end. Coach feeds a new ball immediately after a shot or turnover. The counter happens in the same central space — no separate lane. This keeps the transition immediate and low-scale: recognize the moment, go to the small goal, do not over-organize it.
ProgressionIf central players go wide first every time rather than trying to break centrally, add a condition: play must go through the middle before using a joker. The joker is the release when the central lane is closed, not the first option every time.
Coach Note

The progression condition in this activity directly addresses what showed up on Friday: midfielders abandoning central space and chasing the ball wide. This format can teach that fix without you naming it — the condition makes holding central space the smart play, not a lecture topic. Use it if you see the pattern, hold it if the group is already working the middle first.

Coaching Points
  • Central players: try to break through the middle first — use the joker when the central lane is closed
  • Jokers: set your angle before the ball comes, give a target that makes the return easy to play forward
  • Player who used the joker: move immediately to a new position, do not watch the ball come back
  • Defending team: when you win the ball, recognize the moment and go — the counter puggs are there
  • What opened the chance: the combination, or the defender's mistake?

Free Play  ·  Activity 6 of 6
Free Scrimmage

Format
Free play
Conditions
None
Duration
15–18 min
ObjectiveNo conditions, no touch limits, no jokers. The real test is whether the scanning habit and the combination instinct from tonight hold up once nothing is forcing them. A player who looks for the wall pass in a free scrimmage has started to own the habit. A player who reverts to carrying every time has told you where the next session's coaching points need to land.
Coach Note

Watch, do not coach heavily. The scrimmage is data. Let combinations develop or fail and note which players are still looking for a teammate before receiving. Save your observations for the closing huddle rather than freezing play repeatedly.

Coaching Points
  • Is the scan still there when no condition is forcing it?
  • Who is looking for the combination, and who has gone back to carrying?
  • When a wall pass opens a chance: name it out loud so the group hears what just happened
Closing Huddle
Play It Together
5 minutes questions  ·  5 minutes cool down stretching  ·  Week 1, Session 2
Question 1
When did you feel like the combination actually worked tonight? What made it work?
Listen forPlayers who can name a specific reason, the wall player showed where they wanted it, the first touch went forward, the defender committed one way. That reasoning is the habit starting to form. Players who just say "it went in" or "we passed" haven't connected the action to the outcome yet. Push them: what did you or your teammate do that made it possible?
Question 2
Is there anyone you want to recognize today?
Listen forLet them run it. This is theirs, not yours. If it goes quiet, you can name someone, but give them the space first. The culture of recognizing teammates is built here, not in a game.
